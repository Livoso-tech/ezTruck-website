How COVID-19 Affected Supply Chains and What Comes Next

After several rounds of nationwide lockdowns, the world has changed a lot since the first case of COVID was detected back in 2019. And we are still struggling to go back to the pre-pandemic era. The pandemic has posed a setback in all major sectors and various supply chain systems. As the virus continues to rapidly infect globally, we saw severe shortages in ventilators, masks, toilet paper, medicine, and many more during the initial phase. This was due to a massive failure in the system of the supply chain – an industry that generally networks the suppliers and consumers operating through five steps that are to plan, source, make, deliver, and return. The purpose of this article is to review how supply chains were disrupted and to predict some of the coming trends in the coming years. To reach a conclusive reason, we have to look at the deep-rooted vulnerabilities in the supply chain system, those that most probably were present even before the outbreak.

Negative Impact of COVID on Supply Chains:

The current adversities have brought out the hidden vulnerabilities in supply chains. The disruption showed the lack of flexibility, agility, and efficiency in businesses. Just as a doctor analyses symptoms to identify the disease, we must look closely at the adverse impacts to find out the vulnerabilities.

Disruption of Supply Chains

COVID pandemic brought out the hidden vulnerabilities in supply chains. According to data from Accenture, 94% of businesses suffer from supply chain disruption, 75% of companies had severe negative impacts from and 55% of businesses have downgraded their growth outlooks.

Evolving consumer demands has been a challenge to companies. Companies that were not able to shift to an environment of sustainability and flexibility were out of business. Some companies are still struggling to survive.

The steady-state models were a failure, businesses have now devised a plan that is more customer-centric and solves problems in real-time.

Demand Fluctuation and Supply Shortages

The terms ‘demand fluctuation’ and ‘supply shortages’ are not novel. Companies have been tackling these challenges time and again. During COVID, supply shortage and demand fluctuation both played a key role in disruption.

1. Demand fluctuation is the rise or decline in demand for the product in consumers or retailers.

2. Supply shortages are generally seen when there is a sudden exponential rise in demand from consumers or retailers.

Businesses suffered tremendously during the pandemic. These adversities have made businesses realize the importance of agility, flexibility, resilience, and digitalization. Stores are now open to investing in safety stocks to be prepared for the unprecedented rise in demands and shortages.

Let’s take the example of facemask shortages that occurred in 2020 during the initial period of the COVID outbreak. Before the outbreak, masks were only meant for healthcare workers. After various countries applied the masking strategy, the demand exceeded the supply. Which leads to a shortage. Black marketing was a major problem during COVID

Huge shortages in various products were an opportunity for perpetrators to exploit consumers. As TOI – a leading newspaper conducted a survey that showed the below results.

  • 1. Patients affected by the Corona Virus had to pay 500% more than the usual amount
  • 2. Hospital beds were sold at 200% higher rates
  • 3. COVID related medicines were charged more than MRP
  • 4. Oxygen concentrators, Oxygen cylinders, and refills were sold at higher prices than normal.

What Comes Next?

Businesses will not be going back to the strategies of the pre-COVID era since creating evolved and more efficient strategies remains their primary focus. In 2020, we’ll witness business trends such as agility, sustainability, and customer-driven strategies.

1. The Businesses and Supply Chains Will Adapt to Digita Platforms

There will be a rise in e-commerce in the year ahead. Businesses will focus on developing their presence in digital platforms to enhance their visibility and flexibility. Digital presence not only means using digital platforms to drive in customers but also staying connected with the suppliers Digitalization also helps businesses to understand their customer’s expectations as well as keep them connected with the latest trends and demands.

2. People First Strategies Will Become a Priority

People-first business strategy requires regular upskilling to keep up with the evolving demands of the customers. Customer satisfaction plays a key role in distinguishing a business from the rest. This strategy indicates efficiency and flexibility. Thus, 2022 will witness companies going the extra mile to meet customer expectations.

3. Resilience and Flexibility

“An unsatisfied customer won’t return”.

The last two years of the pandemic have taught businesses the importance of resilience. As a reason, we will see supply chains focusing on increasing their capabilities in mitigating adverse situations. Strategies such as a well-connected network will be a driving force.

To boost the capabilities, businesses will adopt ‘stress tests’ to prepare themselves for an unprecedented event.

4. Keeping Safety Stocks

Learning from the COVID adversities, businesses will invest in keeping a safety stock to fight supply shortages. In 2020, we saw a sudden shortage in everything, even when the shelves on the stores were a bundant with supplies. The global supply chain was disrupted due to long, nationwide lockdowns. Keeping safety stocks is a solution to these sudden shortages.

5. Enhanced Visibility

Visibility will act as a catalyst for growing businesses. As customers climb the ladder of awareness, it is therefore in the best interest of the company to be as transparent as possible. This can be achieved through an online presence. Online presence also helps the business in diving into new markets.

6. Last-Mile Solutions

Smart solutions to last-mile problems can be achieved through the usage of any digital device that helps simplify the last–mile problem. Optimized routes can be selected through modern GPS services. Shipment tracking is another attractive feature for the consumer. Through the implication of customer-centric feedback systems, unique solutions to various last-mile problems can be achieved.

An Outlook on the Future of Supply Chains

After having witnessed the tragedies of COVID, the future of the supply chain will be based on a more localized network of suppliers which will eliminate single-source dependency. The countries that had been depending on other countries such as China and India for manufacturing for cheaper labor will start manufacturing in their own countries. In terms of logistics, a comparison can be made in manufacturing in India and China. China has emerged to be the global hub for manufacturing. From a logistics perspective, China holds an advantage over the latter. Paved roads, railways, waterways, and airways are better in China. On the other hand, India, though has cheap labor costs, it takes more time in delivering goods. Since we are focusing on manufacturing processes, let’s take a look at the manufacturing culture in the two countries. China is keen to deliver a large number of goods on tight schedules whereas India is more focused on manufacturing in small quantities. As a global competitor, China is at an advantage. As companies are now prioritizing building a strong local supply chain network to stay protected against future shocks, several of them have shifted their manufacturing to other Asian countries like Vietnam. Labor costs are no longer cheap in China, but the country is compensating with its advanced technology system. As the digitalization of supply chains is rapidly booming, China has adapted to this new trend firmly. Even though a few companies have downsized by pulling out from China, the future of supply chain and logistics is in China.
